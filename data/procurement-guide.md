# How Procurement Works in Education

## The fundamental truth

Selling to educational institutions is not like selling to businesses. The money is public. The process is designed for accountability, not speed. Understanding this is the difference between closing in 3 months and giving up after 18.

## K-12 District Procurement

### How money flows

- **Federal funds:** Title I, IDEA, ESSER (pandemic relief, winding down). Each funding source has rules about what it can be spent on.
- **State funds:** Per-pupil allocations, categorical grants, textbook adoption budgets
- **Local funds:** Property tax revenue, bond measures, PTA/foundation grants
- **Grants:** Federal (IES, NSF, EIR), state, and private foundation grants

### Budget cycles

- **Planning:** January-March (for next school year)
- **Budget approval:** March-June (board approval of next year's budget)
- **Spending:** July-June (fiscal year in most states)
- **Procurement windows:** The best time to sell is January-April, when next year's budget is being planned. Selling in October means waiting until the following July.

### Purchase thresholds (vary by district)

| Amount | Typical process |
|--------|----------------|
| Under $5K | Principal or department can often purchase directly |
| $5K-$25K | Requires quotes from multiple vendors, department head approval |
| $25K-$100K | Formal bid process, board awareness, IT review |
| Over $100K | Full RFP process, board approval, legal review |

### The actual procurement path

1. **Teacher/admin discovers your product** — demo, conference, colleague recommendation
2. **Pilot request** — they want to try it. Free trial or small paid pilot.
3. **Evidence review** — does it work? What does the data show?
4. **IT security review** — data privacy, FERPA compliance, technical requirements
5. **Budget identification** — which funding source pays for this?
6. **Procurement process** — quotes, bids, or RFP depending on amount
7. **Board approval** (if required by amount)
8. **Contract execution** — legal review of terms
9. **Implementation** — onboarding, training, rollout

### Common blockers

- **No budget allocated:** The money was already committed to other things. Wait for next budget cycle.
- **IT security review:** Can take 2-6 months at large districts. Start this early.
- **Data privacy agreement:** Districts increasingly require signed agreements before any data sharing. Have your template ready.
- **Board calendar:** Boards meet monthly. Missing a board meeting means waiting another month.
- **Change management:** Teachers need training and support. "Just give them the login" doesn't work.

## Higher Ed Procurement

### Budget sources

- **Tuition revenue:** The primary source for most institutions. Under pressure due to enrollment declines.
- **State appropriations:** For public institutions. Varies enormously by state.
- **Grants:** Federal (NSF, IES, NIH), foundation, and internal innovation grants
- **Department budgets:** Individual departments have discretionary budgets, often small
- **Provost/CIO office:** Institutional-level technology budgets for enterprise tools

### Purchase thresholds (vary by institution)

| Amount | Typical process |
|--------|----------------|
| Under $5K | Faculty or department can often purchase on a P-card |
| $5K-$25K | Department approval, possibly IT review |
| $25K-$100K | IT review, procurement office involved, sole-source justification may be needed |
| Over $100K | Formal RFP, multiple vendor evaluation, committee decision |

### The path at higher ed

1. **Faculty champion** — someone uses or discovers your product
2. **Departmental adoption** — multiple faculty use it, department supports it
3. **IT assessment** — security, accessibility, LTI integration, SSO
4. **Procurement negotiation** — pricing, terms, data governance
5. **Institutional adoption** — campus-wide license (the big deal)

### Key differences from K-12

- Individual faculty have more purchasing autonomy (especially for free/low-cost tools)
- The champion model matters more — find a passionate faculty member
- Security reviews are often more rigorous than K-12
- Accessibility (VPAT) is a harder requirement
- Contract terms are negotiated more heavily

## State-Level Adoption

Some states maintain approved vendor lists or conduct state-level textbook/material adoptions.

**State adoption states:** Texas, California, Florida, and about 20 other states have formal adoption processes for instructional materials. Being on the state-approved list dramatically simplifies district purchasing.

**Timeline:** State adoption cycles run every 5-8 years per subject area. The review process takes 12-18 months. Check your target states' adoption schedules.

## Practical Advice for Founders

### Pricing strategies that work

- **Freemium:** Let teachers use a basic version free. Charge the institution for admin features, analytics, and support. This creates bottom-up demand.
- **Per-student pricing:** Most intuitive for education buyers. Common range: $2-$15/student/year for supplemental tools.
- **Site license:** Flat fee for unlimited use at a school or district. Simpler to budget for.
- **Usage-based:** Works for some tools but education buyers generally prefer predictable costs.

### Reducing procurement friction

1. Have your data privacy agreement and VPAT ready before the first sales conversation
2. Offer a free pilot with clear success metrics
3. Align your pricing with common budget thresholds (staying under $25K avoids formal bids at many institutions)
4. Accept purchase orders (not just credit cards)
5. Offer multi-year pricing (institutions prefer budget predictability)
6. Get on state-approved vendor lists where possible
7. Present evidence of effectiveness (see evidence-tiers.md)

### The calendar matters

| Month | What's happening | Your action |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| Aug-Sep | School starts | Support existing customers |
| Oct-Nov | Budget planning begins | Plant seeds for next year |
| Jan-Mar | Budget finalization | Active sales, proposals, demos |
| Apr-May | Purchase orders issued | Close deals |
| Jun-Jul | Fiscal year transition | Implementation and onboarding |
