# EdTech Funding Landscape

## Who Funds EdTech

### Venture Capital (edtech-focused or edtech-active)

**Dedicated edtech investors:**
- Reach Capital — early-stage, education-focused
- Owl Ventures — largest dedicated edtech fund
- NewSchools Venture Fund — focus on equity and access in education
- Rethink Education — early-stage edtech
- Learn Capital — early to growth stage
- GSV Ventures — growth-stage edtech
- Lumos Capital — growth-stage education and workforce

**Generalist VCs active in edtech:**
- Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) — active in AI-for-education
- Bessemer Venture Partners
- Accel
- Tiger Global (growth stage)
- SoftBank (growth stage)

**What VC investors look for in edtech:**
- Large addressable market ($1B+)
- Evidence of product-market fit (usage, retention, revenue growth)
- Path to scale beyond a single institution or district
- Team with education domain expertise AND technical capability
- Clear unit economics or path to them

**What turns VC investors off:**
- "We'll sell to every school" (too broad, no wedge)
- No evidence of effectiveness beyond testimonials
- Over-reliance on grant funding
- Small addressable market with no expansion path
- Founders who don't understand education procurement cycles

### Grants

**Federal:**
- **IES (Institute of Education Sciences):** SBIR/STTR grants for edtech R&D. Phase I: $250K-$300K. Phase II: $900K-$1M. Requires research component.
- **NSF (National Science Foundation):** SBIR/STTR for education technology. Similar structure to IES. Also funds basic research in learning sciences.
- **EIR (Education Innovation and Research):** $3M-$15M grants for evidence-based interventions. Requires ESSA Tier 1-3 evidence depending on grant level.
- **USED (Department of Education):** Various competitive grants for technology in education.

**Foundation:**
- Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation — focus on K-12 math, postsecondary success, equity
- Chan Zuckerberg Initiative — personalized learning, whole child development
- Michael & Susan Dell Foundation — urban education, student success
- Walton Family Foundation — school choice, charter schools
- Arnold Ventures — evidence-based education policy

**Practical guidance on grants:**
- Grants are non-dilutive funding — they don't take equity
- They require significant reporting and compliance overhead
- IES/NSF SBIR is the most founder-friendly grant mechanism
- Don't build a business model dependent on grants — use them to fund research while building a commercial product
- Many grants require a university research partner

## Funding by Stage

### Pre-Seed ($100K-$500K)

**What you need:**
- Clear problem statement with evidence of real pain
- Early prototype or MVP
- Founder with relevant domain expertise
- Target market identified

**Where to look:**
- Angel investors with education background
- Edtech-focused pre-seed funds (Reach Capital, Rethink Education)
- Accelerators (ASU ScaleU, YC, Techstars)
- SBIR Phase I grants
- Friends and family

### Seed ($500K-$3M)

**What you need:**
- Working product with initial users
- Evidence of engagement and early retention
- Clear go-to-market strategy
- Tier 4 evidence minimum, Tier 3 preferred
- Some revenue or clear path to revenue

**Where to look:**
- Edtech-focused seed funds
- Generalist seed funds with edtech interest
- SBIR Phase II grants
- Angel groups and syndicates

### Series A ($5M-$15M)

**What you need:**
- Product-market fit (strong retention, growing usage)
- Revenue ($500K-$2M ARR typical for edtech)
- Tier 3 evidence minimum, Tier 2 strongly preferred
- Repeatable sales process
- Clear expansion path

**Where to look:**
- Owl Ventures, Reach Capital, GSV Ventures
- Generalist VCs with edtech portfolio
- Growth-focused edtech funds

### Series B+ ($15M+)

**What you need:**
- Proven unit economics
- $5M+ ARR with strong growth
- Tier 2 evidence (Tier 1 is a major differentiator)
- National or multi-market presence
- Strong team across functions

## EdTech-Specific Fundraising Advice

1. **Evidence is your moat.** In edtech, evidence of effectiveness is competitive advantage. Investors know that products with strong evidence win procurement decisions and retain customers longer.

2. **Understand the sales cycle.** Edtech has longer sales cycles (6-18 months for institutional sales). Your financial model needs to reflect this. Show investors you understand the cash flow implications.

3. **Don't confuse engagement with outcomes.** "Students love our product" is engagement. "Students using our product scored 20% higher on the state assessment" is outcomes. Investors increasingly demand the latter.

4. **The bottleneck is distribution, not product.** Most edtech startups fail at sales, not product. Show investors you understand how to sell to educational institutions.

5. **Government funding is a double-edged sword.** Pandemic relief (ESSER) created a spending wave that's ending. Products dependent on temporary funding face a cliff. Show investors your product survives budget normalization.

6. **International expansion is often easier than you think.** Education systems share common problems. International markets (UK, Australia, India, Middle East) are often underserved and willing to adopt US-proven solutions.
