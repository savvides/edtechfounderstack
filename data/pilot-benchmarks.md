# Pilot Design Benchmarks

Data and patterns from edtech pilots at research universities and K-12 districts. Use these benchmarks to design effective pilots and set realistic expectations.

## What makes a pilot succeed

### Timeline

- **4-6 weeks:** Too short for meaningful outcome data. Can demonstrate usability, engagement, and teacher/faculty adoption. Acceptable for a proof-of-concept but not for evidence building.
- **8-12 weeks:** The sweet spot for most edtech pilots. Long enough to measure behavior change and preliminary outcomes. Short enough to stay within a single semester.
- **Full semester (15-16 weeks):** Required for rigorous evidence. Allows pre/post assessment with enough instructional time for impact. This is what institutional research teams expect.
- **Full academic year:** Required for Tier 1-2 evidence. Uncommon for initial pilots.

**Benchmark:** Pilots that lead to adoption contracts are most commonly 8-12 weeks. Shorter pilots generate enthusiasm but not evidence. Longer pilots lose momentum.

### Participant scale

- **Minimum viable:** 2-3 classrooms (50-75 students) for qualitative insights
- **Statistically meaningful:** 5-10 classrooms (150-300 students) for quantitative analysis
- **Rigorous study:** 10+ classrooms across multiple schools/departments with comparison group

**Benchmark:** Start with 3-5 classrooms. This gives you enough data to identify patterns while being manageable for support and implementation.

### Success metrics that matter

**Engagement metrics (necessary but not sufficient):**
- Active usage rate (% of enrolled users who actually use the product regularly)
- Session frequency and duration
- Feature adoption patterns
- Support ticket volume and nature

**Benchmark — active usage:** A healthy pilot shows 60-80% weekly active usage among enrolled participants. Below 40% indicates an adoption problem (not a product problem — an implementation problem).

**Outcome metrics (what buyers actually care about):**
- Pre/post assessment scores on targeted skills/knowledge
- Assignment completion rates
- Course grade comparison (pilot vs. non-pilot sections)
- Time-to-competency for specific skills
- Student/learner self-reported confidence or satisfaction

**Benchmark — outcome improvement:** Meaningful pilots show 10-25% improvement on targeted outcomes compared to baseline or comparison. Effects smaller than 5% are hard to distinguish from noise. Effects larger than 30% should be scrutinized for measurement bias.

**Teacher/faculty metrics:**
- Time saved on specific tasks (grading, feedback, content creation)
- Self-reported satisfaction and willingness to continue
- Changes in instructional practice

### Common reasons pilots fail

1. **No faculty/teacher champion:** The pilot was mandated, not volunteered for. Adoption suffers.
2. **Insufficient onboarding:** Teachers/faculty weren't trained on how to integrate the tool into their instruction. They used 10% of the features.
3. **Wrong metrics:** The pilot measured engagement but the buyer wanted outcome data. Or the pilot measured outcomes on the wrong assessment.
4. **Too ambitious:** Trying to prove too much in one pilot. Focus on one or two clear outcomes.
5. **No comparison data:** Without a comparison group or baseline, positive results are anecdotal ("students liked it") not evidence ("students in pilot sections scored 15% higher").
6. **Implementation variability:** Different teachers used the product completely differently. The "treatment" wasn't consistent enough to evaluate.

## Pilot Agreement (MOU) Elements

A Memorandum of Understanding for an edtech pilot should cover:

1. **Objectives:** What specific questions will this pilot answer?
2. **Timeline:** Start date, end date, key milestones
3. **Participants:** Number of classrooms/sections, selection criteria
4. **Implementation plan:** Training schedule, support availability, expected usage
5. **Data collection:** What data will be collected, by whom, how stored, how shared
6. **Success criteria:** Specific, measurable criteria agreed upon in advance
7. **Data privacy:** FERPA compliance, data governance, what happens to data after pilot
8. **Costs:** Who pays for what (product access, researcher time, assessment costs)
9. **IP and publication:** Who owns the findings, can results be published/shared
10. **Exit plan:** What happens at the end — adoption path, data deletion, transition

## IRB Considerations

**When is IRB review needed?**
- If the pilot involves collecting data about human subjects for research purposes (i.e., to generate generalizable knowledge), IRB review is likely required
- If the pilot is purely for internal quality improvement or product evaluation, IRB may not be required
- When in doubt, consult the institution's IRB office — they'll tell you

**Practical guidance:**
- If you plan to publish results or use findings in marketing, get IRB approval. It adds credibility and protects you legally.
- IRB review takes 2-8 weeks for expedited review, 1-3 months for full board review
- Most edtech pilots qualify for expedited review (minimal risk to participants)
- Your university research partner (if you have one) handles the IRB submission

## Pilot-to-Contract Conversion

**Benchmarks:**
- Well-run pilots convert to paid contracts 40-60% of the time
- Pilots with a clear faculty/teacher champion convert at 70%+
- Pilots mandated by administration (no champion) convert at 20-30%
- Pilots with pre-agreed success criteria convert at higher rates because the decision framework already exists

**What increases conversion:**
- Presenting results in the buyer's language (not your metrics, their priorities)
- Having a clear adoption path ready before the pilot ends
- Showing not just "it worked" but "here's what it would look like at scale"
- Addressing implementation concerns proactively (training, support, integration)
